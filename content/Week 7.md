## Academic

### Huron 2006 - Sweet Anticipation

text explores the psychological mechanisms of expectation in human perception, particularly focusing on how they relate to music and emotion. The author details how the brain constantly evolves mechanisms for predicting the future, influencing states like arousal and attention. The text also discusses how the violation of expectations generates specific emotional responses, such as laughter, frisson, and awe, often through the contrast between a fast, negative reaction and a slower, cognitive appraisal.

Huron confirms that Awe is as an emotion in which wonder and fear are combined and is characterized as a "sort of sublime fear". However, to experience awe (a pleasurable state), the threat must be perceived as manageable or encountered in relative safety. Therefore, as proximity to a threat closes in and the danger becomes real and unmanageable, the emotion shifts away from awe (pleasurable, safe fear) back to acute fear or terror.

If the proximity closes in and the danger becomes imminent (e.g. you slip off the secure footing of the cliff edge), the slow appraisal process of "it's manageable" fails, and the instant, negative response (fear) dominates, resulting in a shift away from the pleasure of awe.

Sounds perceived as dissonant, harsh, or noisy engage a defensive reflexes, triggering stress and irritation that the primitive brain interprets as an impending threat, preparing for fight, flight, or freeze.
