Sound in narrative plays a number of roles. In much the same way that lighting can serve both

functional and aesthetic agendas (illuminating characters’ faces on stage but also generating

affective states), sound can similarly interact with a play in both practical and aesthetic ways.

At its most functional, sound articulates diegetic environments (now we’re in a playground/now

we’re in a bedroom) and events (a moth in a jar/the moth jar smashing). More overtly musical

moments can be used to generate audience emotions (a feeling of tenderness as the pair

move towards the kiss/a sense of Sebastian’s growing panic as rhythmic energy starts to

build/ or a sense of weight as Clarissa describes being buried underwater). This

materialisation of events and environments, alongside the articulation of moods can each

contribute to audience engagement. Through its interaction with the text sound can drive

curiosity by making things feel real, or narratively salient whilst also heightening the emotional

stakes. But Moth offers much more. It offers wonder. If curiosity is a narrowing down of focus,

wonder is an opening up, a letting go – being minimised in the face of something beyond your

understanding. “Wonder is to put oneself into the momentum of mystery.” (Vervaeke, 35:16)

This is where sound, for me, is at its most exciting. Whether the valence is positive (wonder,

awe, the sublime) or negative (existential dread, undefined terror),

the _underdetermined_ nature of music – where there is a sense of the _meaningful_ without

actual _meaning_ – makes it a perfect delivery vehicle for such heightened states. And by

building cues in Qlab (the show controller triggering all the sound in the performance) I can

ensure there are layers which deliberately en-strange key moments – where one element is a

deliberate confound and pushes us towards a sense of the unknown.

For example, Saint Sebastian’s first appearance uses the tropes associated with wonder

(tinkly bells to match the tinkly lights, a _Ghost in the Shell_ nod with “exotic” Japanese vocals)

but it also buries backwards screaming saxophones for weird menace and rising shepard’s

tones for anxiety. Using elements to achieve different agendas simultaneously in the mix

opens up a more complex (admittedly subjectively variable) affective space for the audience.

Thinking about how sonic elements interact with each other, the text, performance tone, and

then light opens up a rich possibility space for the audience and avoids “putting a hat on a hat”

(an arguable criticism of blockbuster cinema scores). And whilst lights and sound in this

production are tightly “synchretic” (Chion, 1994), this close synch opened up the chance to

push the audiovisual relationships into more experimental realms. (The low frequencies heard

when Sebastian activates the light globes violates the high sine tones usually associated with

bright light – but we accept this weirdness because of the synchrony.) From an evolutionary

biological point of view, when two senses confirm one event, that event feels more authentic,

salient or impactful. Much of our time in production week was spent more tightly aligning how

sound and light related to each other – crafting a strong audiovisual logic for the characters’

environments, whether diegetic, mystical, psychological or emotional.

At different times the soundtrack was used as either an accelerator or a brake on momentum,

a means by which emotional states could be supporting and shifted, as well as an oscillating

agent to either lock down diegetic space or open up imaginary/supernatural worlds. The

richness of the narrative, the mystery of its logic and the pace at which the narrative unfolds all provided affordances for a soundscape which could amplify the inherent curiosity and

wonder at the heart of the text.

Chion, M, (1994) Audio-vision, Columbia University Press
